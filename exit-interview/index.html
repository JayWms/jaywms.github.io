Not Corporate Property

You are an office worker in a world where corporate culture is the dungeon. You don't fight your way out. You bureaucratic-escape your way out — through clever stealth, system manipulation, and sheer audacity — until you've earned the one privilege that matters: the permanent right to Work From Home.

Where It Comes From

This game exists because a backend engineer asked himself a simple question: can I build something genuinely fun without competing on graphics? The answer is this. A hobby project made by people who've actually sat in open-plan offices, survived mandatory fun days, watched the printer jam at the worst possible moment, and wondered — quietly, from behind a TPS report — how do I get out of here?

The game isn't cynical. It's affectionate. It makes fun of the system, not the people trapped in it. Every mechanic is a joke your co-workers would get. Every NPC is someone you've met.

The best moments in the game won't be scripted. They'll be emergent — when the PM class accidentally triggers the all-hands alarm while the QA Ninja is halfway through the server room, and both players just stare at each other through the split-screen divider. That's the game. That's the feeling we're building toward.

What We're Actually Making
  • 01 — A 2D top-down stealth game in the SNES Zelda visual language — tile-based, readable, and charming rather than flashy.
  • 02 — Four IT-flavored character classes — The Architect, The UI Dev, The QA Ninja, The PM — each with abilities rooted in exaggerated real job skills.
  • 03 — A living office simulation — NPCs with schedules, policies with loopholes, random events that derail every plan. The office actively resists you.
  • 04 — A Focus mechanic that doubles as a co-op camera — watch two parts of the office at once. The screen splits when you separate. Reuniting snaps it back.
  • 05 — A Home Hub you build between levels — ergonomic chair, high-end router, better coffee — until your WFH setup is so good you never have to go back.
  • 06 — A world where the joke is always the system, never the person. Failure is funny. Escape is earned. The satire is the gameplay.
What It Should Feel Like
Playing It
Like the first 20 minutes of a heist movie, except the vault is the server room and the guard is your manager who schedules 4pm Fridays meetings.
When It Works
Like sending a perfect excuse email while your co-op partner sneaks past accounting. You're not fighting the office — you're out-administrating it.
The Humor
Affectionate, not bitter. The fridge notes, the motivational posters, the NPC who sends reply-alls — it's funny because it's true and we survived it.
At Its Best
A stranger plays for 10 minutes and smiles. Not because we made something impressive. Because we made something that felt like their life, and let them win it.
Why I'm Telling You This
We're not looking for employees.
We're looking for collaborators.

This is a slow-burn hobby project. Nobody's quitting their job. Nobody's signing a contract or working weekends they don't want to. The whole point is that we build this the same way the game plays — by outsmarting the constraints rather than fighting them. If you've ever had a terrible job, a great co-worker, a story that made everyone at the table lose it — that's what this game is made of. We have room for people who write, sketch, break things, explain things, or just have opinions. All of it matters. All of it earns a piece of what we ship.
